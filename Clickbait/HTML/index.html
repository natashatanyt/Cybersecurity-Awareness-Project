Clickbait & Misinformation

Introduction

Clickbait articles encourage readers to click on the articles, without regard for the quality of the content, in order to generate revenue for the company. They often appeal to emotion to trick readers into clicking the article.

Some of these articles are intentionally misleading and wrong, with the intent of spreading misinformation, while others are satirical in nature, which mock current affairs.

These articles are becoming increasingly convincing, and young children may be unable to identify fact from fiction. When they come across these articles, they may share articles with friends, believing them to be true.

Avoiding Clickbait

  • Think before you click - Avoid clicking on headlines that shock you
  • Check the source - Some news sites are intentionally satirical
  • Read the article - Check the content before you share

Deepfakes

Introduction

Deepfakes are videos or audio which have been edited to show someoneâ€™s face, voice or body doing something they have not done. These videos can be dangerous as they can spread lies and trick the public.

Deepfakes are becoming increasingly realistic and believable, making it hard to differentiate from reality. These videos can be used to show the victims doing or saying controversial things, which could result in them being targeted and doxxed. Teaching your child about deepfakes reduces the chance that they are fooled, preventing them from spreading misinformation.

Signs of Deepfakes

  • Glitches - rippling/blurring around the neck, eyes, or mouth, and is often more obvious when the person moves or blinks
  • Content - If the content of the video is different from how the person generally behaves, it may be a deepfake
  • Out-of-sync Audio - Lips movements do not match the audio
